Client work.

Seven engagements, in full. Each one a real problem, a system built to solve it, and an honest account of what changed.

01

A café that had run out of levers

Coffee café — growth strategy + digital build. The owner retained me and is opening a second location in the same city.

A neighbourhood café had hit a ceiling. It sold coffee and a few snacks, footfall was flat, and the owner couldn't name a single thing that would move the numbers. Most consultants would hand over a slide deck and leave. I worked it on two levels at once.

On strategy, small moves grounded in how people actually buy: a croissant paired with every coffee to lift the average order, and a corner of seating designed for couples to bring more of them through the door. On the build, the things the café had never had — a website, an AI chatbot to answer customer questions without tying up staff, and a marketing roadmap the owner could run alone after I left.

The point was never a one-off bump. It was to leave the owner with a system and the confidence to run it. Business picked up enough that the owner kept working with me and is now opening a second location in the same city. A client doesn't come back twice for a deck — the repeat engagement is the proof the first one worked.

02

A shop held together by memory

Pharmacy — manual to digital operations. A by-hand business became a searchable one.

A neighbourhood pharmacy ran entirely by hand. Stock lived in the owner's head. Accounts were done manually. Finding a single medicine meant remembering which shelf it sat on — fine until the day it isn't, or the day someone else has to cover the counter.

I replaced the whole manual setup with a system that took over two things at once: the accounting, and the lookup. Now the owner types a medicine name and instantly gets its shelf location and price — type "paracetamol," get the counter and the rate. No gimmicks, no over-engineering; just the two jobs that ate the most time and carried the most risk, done reliably.

What changed isn't only speed. A business that depended entirely on one person's memory became something that could be searched, checked, and handed to someone else. That's the difference between a shop and an operation.

03

Outreach that no longer caps out at one caller

Study-abroad edtech platform — autonomous AI sales agent. Up to 2× more students reached per day, no added headcount. In ongoing production.

A study-abroad edtech platform was acquiring students the hard way — one person finding prospects and cold-calling them, one at a time. That model has a hard ceiling: however many calls a human can make in a day is the most students you can ever reach.

I built and deployed an autonomous AI sales agent that runs the entire outreach chain on its own. It finds prospective students, calls them, qualifies each one by their learning goals, and recommends courses that actually fit — the full sequence a good rep would run, without the human bottleneck.

The result is capacity the platform couldn't have bought without hiring. It now reaches up to 2× more students per day with no added headcount, and the agent runs in ongoing production rather than as a demo. The interesting part isn't that it's AI. It's that outreach stopped being a function of how many hours one person had.

04

Getting the founder's time back

Travel agency — operations automation. Routine operations run on their own. Live and in ongoing use.

The founder of a travel agency was drowning in repetitive manual work — the kind of tasks that fill a day without moving the business — and had no technical background to build his way out.

I started by watching how the business actually ran, then isolated the specific tasks eating his time. From there I built an automation pipeline: Claude handling the analysis, Notion giving it structure, and Zapier wiring the routine work so it runs without him touching it.

The system now handles his day-to-day operations. What that really buys him is attention — the parts of a small business that genuinely need the founder finally get his hours, instead of the parts a machine can do just as well. It's live and in ongoing use.

05

A feed that finally shows up every day

Jewellery e-commerce brand — AI content engine. Orders up ~25% and about 2,000 new followers in the months since launch.

A jewellery e-commerce brand had the common problem behind soft sales: low visibility, weak engagement, and nothing being posted consistently. The bottleneck wasn't taste — it was throughput. Good content is work, and the work wasn't happening.

I built a daily AI content pipeline that handles creation and publishing end to end. Every morning it generates the visuals, assembles a post, and publishes to the brand's Instagram automatically — a steady organic presence with no manual effort. Paid advertising was deliberately left out of scope, so none of this is bought reach.

In the months since the system went live, the brand's orders are up roughly 25% and its following has grown by about 2,000, alongside a daily presence it never had before. I won't claim the content alone drove every sale — but the brand went from silent to showing up every single day, and the numbers moved with it.

06

The money that expired on the shelf

Regional grocery retailer — AI inventory intelligence. Dairy spoilage down 40%, measured before and after. Still in use.

A regional grocery retailer was losing money to a problem quiet enough to ignore: perishables expiring on the shelf before they sold. Dairy was the worst of it — short shelf life, easy to over-order, and nothing in the store actively watching for it.

I built an AI inventory system that flags perishables approaching expiry and recommends reorder quantities, so the store stops over-buying exactly the items that spoil fastest. The scope was deliberately tight: get dairy right first, where the bleeding was worst.

Dairy spoilage fell 40% — measured before the system and after, not estimated — and it remains in ongoing use. It's an unglamorous win, which is the kind small businesses feel most: less product in the bin every week, straight back to the margin.

07

A capital decision that stopped being a guess

Home-textiles manufacturer — location intelligence for capital allocation. A defensible basis for where and how much to invest; the model is reused for each new plant.

A home-textiles manufacturer — bedsheets, curtains, rugs, covers — faced one of the most expensive decisions a business makes: whether to build a new plant, where to put it, and how much to sink into it. Get it wrong and the mistake is measured in years.

I treated it as a location-and-investment problem rather than a hunch. That meant a city-wide analysis of demand and competitor positioning, then an AI model that assesses whether a new plant is warranted at all and identifies the strongest location and investment level. I didn't hand over a yes or a no — I handed over a way of deciding.

The client moved from guesswork to a defensible basis for the call, and now reuses the model every time a new-plant question comes up. That's the real return: the framework outlives the single decision.
